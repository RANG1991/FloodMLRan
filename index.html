Deep Learning For Regional Spatio-Temporal Streamflow Prediction

Ran Galun (Under the supervision of Prof. Efrat Morin)
The Hebrew University of Jerusalem
Code

Abstract

In spite of the fact that precipitation-runoff modeling has been extensively studied, it remains a challenging problem in the hydrological sciences today. Even when considering a single basin, traditional models have difficulty making accurate predictions, and the problem becomes even more acute when considering a region with several basins simultaneously (i.e. regional modeling).
The use of deep learning based approaches have recently improved the accuracy of streamflow discharge predictions for a large number of basins with heterogeneous characteristics. However, the models proposed so far did not take into account the spatial complexity of the different hydrological attributes, and specifically of precipitation which its spatial variability is known to has a major effect on runoff properties. Taking advantage of these spatial complexities could increase the accuracy of the predictions and make the models more robust.
In this work, we present an approach for regional streamflow discharge prediction using 2 models, CNN-LSTM and CNN-Transformer models.
We trained and evaluated these models using spatial precipitation data along with other dynamic and static attributes of 135 basins in the US from the CAMELS data set, and demonstrated their potential to provide even more accurate predictions.
We used as a baseline the current state-of-the-art LSTM model for regional streamflow discharge prediction, which takes into account only mean areal precipitation, in contrast to the 2 spatial models.
An analysis of the influence of the dynamic and static attributes on the prediction is also presented, as well as an analysis of the spatial data used as an input to the CNN-LSTM model.
We found that there is no significant advantage of the 2 models on the current state-of-the-art LSTM model. However, both of the models were found to be sensitive to both dynamic and static attributes related to precipitation spatial variability.
